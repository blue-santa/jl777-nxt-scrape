So every loan is always fully backed by BTSX. In your example, you were imagining that bitUSD is some kind of leveraged USD; it's the other way around!

So you see the collateral requirements are extremely conservative. Why would I put up $200 to borrow $100, you ask? So that I can sell the bitUSD, hoping BTSX will appreciate relative to USD. Can you explain what will happen if a whale or group of whales all short the bitUSD at the same time.

Let's say I'm a whale: I put up all my BTSX as collateral and short bitUSD, flooding the market with bitUSD. As a result, the price of bitUSD temporarily plunges way below that of USD. Hundreds of opportunistic traders rush in to buy the bitUSD at a discount, knowing that the price will rise back to that of USD. Of course, this causes the price of bitUSD to rise, which could re-establish the bitUSD/USD peg all by itself.

However, simultaneously, other traders fear that the peg is failing, so they rush out and convert BTSX to "real" USD, which decreases the value of BTSX relative to USD. Note that this is the opposite direction that the whale thought the price would go! So the whale's strategy has worked against him, and if the real USD price of BTSX falls too much, the network will execute a margin call, confiscating his BTSX collateral and using it to repurchase all the BitUSD he originally shorted. Thus, in the end, the loan is repaid and the whale is beached without a dime to his name.

I've left out some implementation details, specifically that the market price at which someone is allowed to short is not naively the last price or anything like that; I mean it's a moving average of market prices over some time period. I don't know these exact details, but there are quite a few "safety" features built into it to prevent really destructive attacks.
