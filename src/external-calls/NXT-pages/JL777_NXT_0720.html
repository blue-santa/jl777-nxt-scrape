I confirmed that as long as an unconfirmed tx is alive, the assets + NXT it needs are locked, at least I cant submit a new transaction that uses it. so this eliminates double spends pretty effectively and also means that any transaction in the unconfirmed tx list is fully funded. this is a qualitatively stronger commitment for the trade than just having signed tx bytes via offchain, even if all basic tests are passed.

This is because a rogue client could send out multiple offchain signed tx all using the same assets. So it needs to be a requirement that all dependent tx to be submitted to the network and appear in the unconfirmed tx list

The edge cases are:
a) predicted time for a block is wrong and not only that just happens to come right between the expiration times of the different legs. Since all these tx are created within seconds, that means that the unexpectedly long block needs to hit a precise window whose width is a few seconds

b) attacker forges a block and only includes the legs of the trade he likes and then empties his acct so the next block wont just confirm the pending tx. Now he cant make a block for 1440 blocks.

Due to my previous estimates, b) appears to be a lot of work for making gains via slippage. If you just want to make slippage gains, seems much less work and much more consistent to become a market maker. For a) with jnxt.org predictions of the next 2 blocks having a pretty good success rate, I would be surprised to see any such thing happening very often at all.

If an edge case can occur naturally, it can also be engineered, and will be engineered if there is a financial incentive to do so (which there is or will be), in which case all bets are off in terms of how often this will occur.

Quote from: jl777 on March 05, 2015, 08:36:22 pm
Also if you are an arbitrageur then unfilled legs are part of the business, if you are not willing to accept a small risk of this, then you shouldnt be arbitraging.
Risk is part of the business, but only in its predictable variety as far as arbitrage is concerned. As an arbitrageur, you'll enter a trade if the gain expectation is high enough after taking into account the gain you stand to make on the arbitrage if it works, the probability that the second leg won't get filled and you have to roll back the first leg, and the cost of rollbacking the first leg by crossing the spread not to mention more complex situation where you would consider delaying the rollback.

There is a huge difference in terms of risk between a two-leg trade and a three leg trade, in particular when two legs out of three turn out to be in a low liquidity / large spreads market and it takes up to 1mn to confirm that the first two legs are in. If you allow for edge cases to happen, knowing that they will happen increasingly frequently because there is an incentive to engineer them, you are making the expectation of loss higher for arbitrageurs, and pricing them out for most trades but the juiciest where they would stand to gain more than they risk to lose.

You could say farewell, we don't need arbitrageurs, they are only taking liquidity, but that would be ignoring the fact that most market makers are also arbitrageurs, so by making arbitageurs life more difficult you are doing yourself a disservice in the long run.

Quote from: jl777 on March 05, 2015, 08:36:22 pm
You are also now stretching the risk by assuming that we will see price movements of 10% in 10 seconds. Exactly how often does this happen?
I haven't given any estimate on the volatility level that would make it a problem for market participants. Arbitageurs for one are making tiny profits on many small transactions. Depending on the profit margin you are looking at for a trade, variations in double or single digit basis points can make the difference between a profit and a loss. This is alright when you already accounted for historical volatility in your trade, but much less ok when you get some extra uncalled for leg coming from nowhere and have to take a 1mn exposure to the market volatility before you can do anything about it.

Quote from: jl777 on March 05, 2015, 08:36:22 pm
If you trade forex, is it guaranteed that your trade is filled?
No, but forex is extremely liquid and has tiny spreads so you can easily model the cost of not getting filled, and there are plently of methods to evaluate the probability that you'll be filled. That's a whole different situation than ending up after 1mn with an asset you didn't ask for, and have to roll it to your target asset or close the position in a low liquidity market with large spread.

Quote from: jl777 on March 05, 2015, 08:36:22 pm
If you put in a market order is your price guaranteed?
You don't want to send market orders for you first leg in a low liquidity / wide spread / high volatility market when you are doing arbitrage. So bad or good example depending on how you look at it.

makeoffer has failure modes that expose it to risk and so it will not be supported for GUI invoked usage. Please do not use this API call. There is nothing I can do about the NXT protocol as I am not a NXT core dev. I just write software that uses NXT.

So we can limit the discussion to makeoffer2, which is far more robust.

If you are thinking that somebody would setup an attack to gain from slippage of unexecuted half of arbitrage, then this is not very practical. What is the economic gain from this? let us say a 1 BTC arbitrage pair, one half isnt filled. The market moves and you cover for a 0.05 BTC loss. Not a great result, but this is the edge case. Now let us calculate the cost to the attacker. We ignore his time to create the attacking software, but he need to forge a block to be able to do this attack. Now if he has 50 million NXT forging for him, he will get ~1 in 10 blocks. So that is about 150 blocks forged for a 1440 block period. After conducting this attack, he will lose the ability to forge for 1440 blocks.

Recently the average block is getting around 4 NXT per block * 150 = 600 NXT expected loss. The gain of 0.05 BTC is around 1000 NXT, so there is a gain for 400NXT in this case. But we need to remember the attack is making money due to slippage, so it could just as easily go the other way. Let us assume the attacker is clever enough to complete the trade if it slips against him, but now we have cut the expected return in half to ~500NXT per attack.

These are very rought estimates, but I dont think a 1BTC trade size and 5 % slippage are too far from the expected sizes. So that means this is appearing to be a lot of work for a breakeven outcome. Now would I like it if this is not possible to happen under any circumstance? Of course, but I am not a NXT core dev so I cannot do anything about this.

Now if we can assume the edge case is < 0.01% of the cases it will have a minimal impact on profitability. I am not quite ready to offer insurance against this scenario, but once we get some actual data, if it is any actual issue then we can make an insurance to cover such slippage losses caused by this edge effect.

The edge effect is really more a theoretical possibility as all the transactions are already in the unconfirmedtransactions list. So either they all execute, or they dont. And within seconds of firing off a trade you will know if it was accepted or rejected. Granted you wont be able to retrade what you just obtained until the block is confirmed, but presumably you have an inventory much greater than any single trade, so as long as you arent trading 100% of your inventory this wont really affect you.

InstantDEX is a decentralized peer to peer system and I am not a NXT core dev. Given these constraints the makeoffer2 is the best I can do and if this is not good enough, then I can understand that you wont use it. However you seem like a smart enough guy who can factor in the amount of losses from the edge effect that is based on actual statistics and this statistic will be right there on the blockchain as each trigger tx has all the info needed to corrolate all the dependent tx.

You say "your architecture and design decisions won't fly and that you are letting large avenues open for things to go horribly wrong" and this seems to me to be assuming that my analysis that the attack vector is about breakeven is incorrect. And also that slippage of one trade per day is a horrible thing. Remember the attacker loses the abiity to attack for 1440 blocks after each attack. I cant believe that this is something so horrible that you cant factor it into your bid/ask spreads. I claim that it wont make financial sense to do these attacks, so we wont see them much at all. But even if there is an active attacker it is happening a few times per week and the downside is slippage.

Compared to trading forex where slippage against the house is the norm and where the trade fee is a fixed 3 cents and there is no centralized point of failure, it seems a good tradeoff to me. The technical differences that are necessary will require customizations in your risk assessment module, along with fixed price fees and you can even make a list of accounts that you wont trade against because they attacked you. At least you are in control of all bids and asks and who you are trading with.

So we can agree to disagree that setting up the attack makes any financial sense. If you are right that means about 4 trades per week will suffer from slippage. If I am right, more like one trade per TBD and it is a non issue.

James

makeoffer flow is:
1) initiator sends info about the trade including reftx hash to the other node

2) other node verifies it matches one of its open orders and if it does it sends signed tx that depends on the trigger back to the originating node.

3) the originating node verifies what came back matches what it expected and if all is good, it submits the trigger and the other party's tx to the network.

makeoffer2 flow is:
a) initiator sends info about the trigger tx to the other node and the jump node if it is a hybrid trade, it also submits dependent transactions to the network.

b) the other node(s) verify the incoming order is acceptable and they submit their dependent transaction to the network.

c) the original node checks to see if all the dependent transactions are in the unconfirmed transactions and that they are what is expected. If so, it submits the trigger, which when confirmed triggers all the other transactions.

So the answer is I do it both ways!
And the dependent transactions that are sitting in unconfirmed transactions dont incur a fee if they are not executed. So not only is there no fee for submiting bids and asks, unless the transaction is confirmed, there is no fee, even if you start a sequence or are involved in one.
