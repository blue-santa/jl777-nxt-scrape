the formula for the probability is not finalized yet, but I am leaning toward a proportional ratio like you write, but for a moving window in time. Not sure the timewindow yet, but something 3 to 6 months. Maybe some sort of weighed moving average.

narrowcast latency is the direct ping time between the two nodes. broadcast would depend on the route the packet ends up taking. I am using both BTCD and SuperNET networks for the propagation.

Each peer will decide locally what node it wants to do a trade with. So barring some user specific filter, there is no need for "authority to decide which came in first" as your node will clearly know which packet it got first. More practically, if an offer comes in that matches a pending offer you have, it will mark that offer as pending and so only one can be pending at a time per offer you have. After a timeout (10 to 20 seconds) if for whatever reason the trade is not finalized, then it goes back into the available pool. Though I just realized I didnt flip this bit yet! Thanks, thats a bug fixed.

NXT has a referencedtransaction field for all orders. So I create a set of transactions that are depending on a trigger order. The node that starts the makeoffer2 API contacts the other node(s) about the trade it wants to do (based on existing orderbook entries) and gives them the information needed so the counterparty(s) can create dependent transactions. Once all parties are satisfied that all parts of the trade are acceptable, it is up to the originating node to publish the trigger tx, which when confirmed causes the dependent transactions to confirm.

So while technically these transactions are not confirmed until the next block, since all the parties are verifying that everything is properly signed for the proper amounts and it is published to the blockchain, it is just a matter of time for them to be confirmed. It is like after you click on Send Money and it hits the blockchain, the money it is spent. Some extreme edge cases make it possible for some of the dependent transactions to not get confirmed, but since all the parts are marketrate swaps the financial exposure is minimal for these edge cases. With some additional checks I will be able to get the edge cases to be extremely unlikely

I choose a clearing price at the median of high bid and low ask on the orderbook and since both sides are adding equal liquidity on both sides, even if there are other orders, it all just clears. Of course if at the very block somebody is submitting an order at the exact same price then it could end up leaving one half of the trade partially filled. For assets with many decimals this would be rare, but for low decimals assets, it is an issue and I think using the older makeoffer method would be required. That doesnt use the AE so there is a small risk that the block generator can be the one you are transacting with, I think for small value trades this is acceptable as I could probably even insure against the small losses.

The risk with connecting to untrusted nodes is they could potentially feed you bogus information, such as telling you that you have received funds when in actual fact you haven't.  But for this to work, the hacker also has to be the person you are trading with and also control a bunch of nodes and hope that your NxtLite client uses his nodes.

This validity checking is pretty primitive at the moment and could be improved upon, but for the meantime if you trust the person you are trading with not to have an army of hacked public nodes then your pretty safe.  If you are trading a large amount and are suspicious of the other party then perhaps double check your incoming tx against one of the block explorer websites (takes a few seconds) or just wait for a few more confirmations from other public nodes.
