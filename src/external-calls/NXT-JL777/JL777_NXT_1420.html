I think I am taking your suggested approach.

For instance, the current entrant sent in 10 NXT to NXTRaffle, ensuring him spots 0-9.
The TX'id is 8185374561532548078, so, I will use the first 8 (81853745), eventually to be summed up with the TX'ids of the other entrants and eventually mod the sum with the total number of entrants.
Sound correct?
I do not know if the sum of txids has any sort of pattern that is provably random, especially if it is the txids that are being submitted by the contestants.

since the contestants can control what tx they submit (eg. just keep generating but not broadcasting tx until they get one with properties they like), just allowing that to be part of the random output just seems to be contaminating it. Maybe you can prove it isnt, but why not use something that cant be controlled at all by the contestants?

I think having T spots that correspond to the total NXT coming in is ok. However, to be fair, the random number used to determine the winner should be 0 mod T to avoid biasing the earlier entries, unless you want to give preference to the early entries. With a large 64 bit number, it wont really matter much as it is diluted, but the smaller the random number is, the bigger this effect.

For an extreme case, lets assuming random number is up to 20 and you have 18 spots. then R mod 18 would have a very large bias in favor of spots 0 and 1

Not sure why you dont just do a SHA256(most recent N blockhash) mod T
where the SHA256 can be truncated to the least significant 64bits so you can use 64 bit math
just concatenate N blockhashes and take SHA256 of it. With a cutoff at a certain blockheight. Each new block coming in will change the outcome and only the one that forges the latest block can experiment until it has the right value, but I think we can assume this is a small risk.

If you want to make it immune to even that, then also use the blockhash of the BTC block that comes in after the critical blockheight to make it so both the BTC miner and NXT forger have to cooperate.

James

P.S. Depending on when you need your random number, you might be able to just use the SuperNET provably random number API that I am working on
