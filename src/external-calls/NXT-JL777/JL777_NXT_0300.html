You've touched on an important point. Did you know that the first JetSki - Bombardier's Sea-Doo - was introduced in the late 1960s?? And flopped to the point that Bombardier shelved the project until the close of the 1980s?

Compared to that adoption curve, we're pretty fortunate. The trouble with truly disruptive innovations is that they break habits - and ordinary people do like their habits. Case in point: video phones. AT&T's Bell Labs had an up-and-running vidphone at the same time Bombardier was selling the original Sea-Doo. Its vidphone sank because people had a habit of talking on the phone in their "nightgowns" or "housecoats". Their habits had made them expect visual privacy when yakking away. On the business side, the vidphone faced a catch-22: "Why would I want that? If I want to speak to someone, I'll just use the regular phone. If the matter is important enough for me to see his body language, then it's important enough for me to meet with him face to face." So, the vidphone was shelved until Skype came along.

Compared to those two disruptors, we're doing quite well. Nxt is being adopted now, even though it's less than two years old. From the perspective of innovative history, we're moving at a very fast rate.
