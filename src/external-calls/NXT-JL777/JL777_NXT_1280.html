Nxt General Discussion / Re: A solution to public key debacle
« on: December 07, 2014, 07:28:10 pm »
Quote from: Brangdon on December 07, 2014, 03:44:32 pm
Quote from: benjyz on December 07, 2014, 03:06:44 pm
what does "likely" refer to here? 1/2**64 = 1/18446744073709551616
It means a collision could theoretically be manufactured within a reasonable time frame. Not many people would be comfortable using 64-bit security only.
It is like the birthday paradox. It is surprising how few people you need to have in a group before there is a birthday collision.

Q1: How many people in a group before there is a 50% chance of collision?
Q2: What is the chance of collision with 75 people in a group?

I made a little page about this a while back: http://betterexplained.com/articles/understanding-the-birthday-paradox/

The answer to Q1 is 23! But the more troublesome answer is 99.9% for Q2!!

While trying to crack a specific account is quite a bit more difficult, when you are wanting to avoid ANY collision, there are just so many cases, it rapidly becomes a reality. Odds are the two accts that end up colliding will be small ones, but still, it is better to have a bigger space.

James

P.S. So the large looking 1/2**64 = 1/18446744073709551616, is actually much smaller when the question is avoiding ALL collisions.

Market making is used in virtually all real world markets. Not that that justifies doing it, but the concept is that in exchange for providing liquidity (ability to buy or sell close to NAV) the market maker gets to make the difference between bids and asks.

So instead of relying on someone to be selling at close to NAV when you want to buy, or the reverse, the market maker is providing this service.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_maker

Even the SEC is treating market makers as normal business: http://www.sec.gov/answers/mktmaker.htm

James

complex field, no kidding !  My gripe with this was just because i picture jl777 as a person, who is only interested in real valuations, but, i now see, its a common technique, which seems to be necessary. Thanks.
strictly speaking it isnt necessary
however, unless the market is naturally very active, not having a market maker can and often leads to trading well below value. If you think about it, it makes sense. Given the choice to be invested in something where you can cashout at close to its value (eg. it has a market maker) vs. having to wait for a counterparty (no market maker and virtually all altcoins and assets), all things being equal you would happily let the market maker make a bit of profits.

So, having an active market maker actually makes something worth more and of course the bid/ask spread is smaller and the price is closer to NAV.
