Nxt Helpdesk / Re: How secure is the automatically generated 12 word passphrase?
« on: January 31, 2015, 08:22:04 pm »
Quote from: Bernard Lerring on January 31, 2015, 05:43:27 pm
I realise that this has probably been asked before, but if I'm going to be storing 5 figures worth of Nxt in my account I'd like to know what the probability of getting bruteforced is.

Would it be worth opening up a new account and setting my own strong password and then transferring everything over to the new account? Am I secure to carry on using the generated one? I already store my current passphrase encrypted with a good password, which I have to enter before I can see it.

Are there any statistics which tell me how long it would take for a hacker to bruteforce a standard Nxt 12 word passphrase? Is it made public how many words are in the Nxt passphrase database?

Thanks if you can help me sleep confident at night!
The odds are extremely small for any bruteforcing. A much bigger danger is human error or somebody in your inner circle somehow getting access. Social engineering things. Some cute girl rings your doorbell (happens to me all the time!) asking for some sugar...

So, to protect against these sorts of things, if you are really paranoid, you can make ~10 accts. 9 of these will be "cold" and accessed just once per month. They will all have around 10% of your holdings, so no single acct represents catastrophic loss. Then have a smallish acct for day to day usage. protect the passwords with multiple encrypted offline copies, or paper printout in a bank vault if you have access to such. (might not be a bad idea for legacy handover to inheritors to have paper instructions in a known place to them).

OK, now to operate at the highest levels of safety you need a dedicated computer (doesnt have to be powerful, just dedicated for this and offline). A fresh OS install, never connected to the internet. copy the entire nxt folder from a computer that has seen your accts (this way they know about the public keys). Now you can do offline transactions for all the "cold" accounts.

it is a bit of a hassle, but you generate the signed transaction bytes, copy to USB. and then load the USB from your day to day computer and submit them to the network.

The above is NOT necessary, but if you follow something like the above it protects against social engineering attacks, keyloggers, but still no defense against those cute girls ringing your bell.
